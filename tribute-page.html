Nikola Tesla

A injusticed man

Timeline

  • 1856 - Was born to a Serbian family in the village Smiljan, Lika County, in the Austrian empire.

  • 1861 - Attended primary school in Smiljan, where he studied German, arithmetic and religion.

  • 1870 - Completed elementary school, followed by high school.

  • 1873 - Completed high school, and returned to his family in Smiljan where he contracted a terrible cholera, where he had spent nine months in bed.

  • 1875 - After recovering, he enrolled at the Austrian Polytechnic in Graz, Austria. He was a notable student, with excellent grades, but he started playing games of chance which hurt him a lot by not completing his studies.

  • 1881 - Moved to Budapest, Hungary, to work with Tivadar Puskás in a telegraph company. He was assigned to the position of chief electrician.

  • 1882 - Tivadar Puskás got another job for Tesla in Paris at the Continental Edison Company. He started working in what was then a new industry, installing indoor incandescent lighting across the city in the form of electricity.

  • 1884 - Migrated to the United States, becoming a naturalized citizen, to work at Edison Machine Works for about six months.

  • 1885 - Due to some disagreements, Tesla left the company and founded his own company financed by two entrepreneurs, Robert Lane and Benjamin Vail, Tesla Eletric Light & amp, Manufacturing, where he manufactured arc lighting lamps some of his patents, such as a DC generator enhanced.

  • 1886 - When the company went into operation, and the entrepreneurs were little interested in their projects, they left Tesla penniless, leading to bankruptcy. Tesla had to lose control of his created patents assigned to the company in exchange for shares. He had to work on various electrical repair jobs and even as an excavator.

  • 1887 - He founded his own Tesla Eletric Company with the help of two New York lawyers experienced in starting companies interested in his ideas and supported him financially. Developed an induction motor that operated with alternating current (AC)

  • 1888 - Licensed and patented its alternating current induction motor

  • 1889 - The money Tesla earned from licensing his AC patents made him independent and wealthy and gave him time and money to pursue his own interests. He traveled to the 1889 Universal Exhibition in Paris and learned about Heinrich Hertz's experiments from 1886 to 1888, which proved the existence of electromagnetic radiation, including radio waves. After the exhibition, he created what turned out to be the Tesla coil.

  • 1891 - At the age of 35, he became a naturalized citizen of the United States.

  • 1893 - In St. Louis, Missouri, Tesla told viewers that he was sure that a system like his could eventually conduct "intelligible signals or perhaps even power at any distance without the use of wires", leading him across the Earth, as well it would make it possible for the entire planet to receive electricity.

  • 1895 - Edward Dean Adams, impressed by what he saw when he visited Tesla's laboratory, agreed to help found The Nikola Tesla Company, created to finance, develop and commercialize a variety of Tesla's previous patents and inventions, in addition to the new ones.

  • 1894 - Began to investigate what he called "invisible" types radiant energy after observing damaged films in his laboratory in previous experiments (later identified as "Roentgen rays" or "X-rays")

  • 1898 - He introduced a boat that used a radio control based on a cohesor - which he dubbed "telautomaton" - to the public during an electrical exhibition at Madison Square Garden.

  • 1899 - He set up a high-altitude experimental station in Colorado Springs, to further study the conductive nature of low-pressure air.

  • 1906 - Due to lack of investment, his project to be able to transmit electric energy wirelessly led him to have a nervous breakdown. He had to mortgage his property to cover his debts.

  • 1915 - November 6 A report by the Reuters news agency in London said that the 1915 Nobel Prize in Physics would be awarded to Thomas Edison and Nikola Tesla; however, on November 15, a Reuters report from Stockholm stated that the prize that year would be awarded to Sir William Henry Bragg and William Lawrence Bragg "for their services in analyzing the crystal structure using X-rays".

  • 1943 - On January 7 he died alone in room 3327 of the New Yorker Hotel. His body was later found by maid Alice Monaghan after she entered Tesla's room, ignoring the "do not disturb" sign he had placed on his door two days earlier. Assistant medical examiner H. W. Wembley examined the body and decided that the cause of death had been coronary thrombosis.
